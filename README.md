## Background

We focus specifically on burnout-related procrastination, not clinical burnout treatment. QuestLog does not treat burnout clinically; it supports motivation through behavioral design inspired by reward-system research.

Academic burnout functions as a chronic state of exhaustion that downregulates dopamine receptors, transforming educational challenges into a source of dread rather than achievement. This chemical imbalance leads to a profound sense of detachment and cognitive decline, causing a sharp drop in academic performance and a rise in long-term psychological distress. Ultimately, this cycle compromises a student's mental health and self-esteem, threatening both their current graduation rates and their future professional trajectory.

### Gamify?

*Prompt: give me a definition for Gamify*

To gamify is to apply game-design elements and principles (such as point scoring, competition, and rules of play) to non-game activities to increase engagement and motivation.

### RPG?

The term RPG, or role-playing game, originates from table-top games such as Dungeons and Dragons and Cyberpunk. These games allowed players to "role-play" as characters in another world and usually involved leveling systems, skills, quests, and character building. With the advent of computer gaming, the core concepts were adapted for the new medium. Examples of well-known RPGs include Pokemon, Stardew Valley, Final Fantasy, and Terraria.

- Burnout and procrastination are not caused by laziness, but by loss of motivation and emotional energy
- The real problem is not task management, but motivation management
- Games are one of the few systems proven to sustain motivation without external pressure

**Why RPG specifically?**

RPGs are uniquely suited for this problem.

- 🎮 **Progression-based** — XP, levels, and quests give visible, continuous progress
- 🎯 **Small actions matter** — Even minor tasks contribute to long-term growth
- 🧠 **Restores self-efficacy** — Users feel capable again by "winning" small battles
- 🔁 **Intrinsic motivation loop** — Effort → reward → progress → desire to continue
  - 👉 This directly counteracts burnout-driven disengagement

- Gamification is already accepted and normalized (Duolingo, fitness apps)
- Users are familiar with RPG concepts — no learning curve
- When motivation is broken, systems that rely on motivation fail. RPG mechanics rebuild motivation by turning effort into visible progress.

Burnout in modern college students is increasingly recognized not just as "laziness" or "tiredness," but as a professional-grade health crisis that fundamentally alters the brain's reward systems.

**Professional Sources and Statistics (2024-2025)**

- **The 80% Threshold:** A 2024 survey of the "Class of 2024" found that over 80% of students experienced burnout at some point during their degree, with nearly 30% feeling it "frequently" (Higher Ed Dive / Handshake 2024).
- **Retention Crisis:** Data from the Lumina Foundation and Gallup (2024/2025) indicates that one in three students considers leaving their program due to emotional stress, making burnout the leading predictor of college dropouts.
- **The Flourishing Gap:** While acute anxiety symptoms have slightly declined, the 2024-2025 Healthy Minds Study found that "flourishing" — a measure of purpose and optimism — dropped to 36%, suggesting that even students who aren't "clinically ill" are struggling to feel motivated.
- **Dopamine Deficiency:** Clinical research from the Cleveland Clinic (2024) and MDPI (2025) links chronic burnout to "blunted" dopamine pathways, where the brain effectively stops producing the "drive" necessary to complete tasks.

**Examples of Why Burnout is a Problem**

The following illustrate how burnout and dopamine dysfunction specifically harm students:

- **Neurochemical Paralysis:** Chronic academic stress causes "dopamine downregulation," meaning students literally lack the chemical motivation to start assignments even when they know the deadline is critical.
- **The Anhedonia Loop:** Burnout leads to anhedonia (the inability to feel pleasure), causing students to withdraw from social clubs and hobbies that used to provide the "reward" needed to balance their stress.
- **Decision-Making Fatigue:** High cortisol from burnout weakens connections in the prefrontal cortex, which leads to "careless mistakes" and an inability to manage complex schedules or long-term projects.
- **The "Anti-Reward" State:** Research shows that in a burnt-out brain, the "effort" of studying feels infinitely higher while the "reward" of a good grade feels meaningless, leading to chronic, guilt-ridden procrastination.
- **Physical Health Cascades:** Beyond mental fog, the "occupational phenomenon" of burnout triggers physical symptoms like chronic fatigue and weakened immunity, leading to increased absenteeism and lower GPA.

## Approach

From your key (major) references (journals), what will your solution help advance the research for this problem space?

Our contribution is translating burnout and motivation research into a low-friction, game-based system that rewards effort consistently without judgment.

- XP is measured by time spent, making rewards objective and easy to understand
- Time-based XP reduces decision fatigue and self-judgment
- Class-based bonuses add meaning when tasks align with a character's role
- The system rewards effort, not difficulty or perfection
- Small amounts of time still count, encouraging users to start and stay engaged

**Daily streaks: YES, but redesigned**

❌ Traditional streaks (Duolingo-style)
- Miss one day → streak resets
- Creates anxiety
- Encourages quitting after failure

✅ Burnout-safe streaks (what you should do)
- "Momentum streak", not "perfect streak"
- Missing a day does not erase progress
- Example: 3 days active → +10% XP; miss a day → streak pauses, doesn't break; resume → bonus continues
- 👉 This rewards return, not perfection.

**Gentle decay (not loss)**
- Buffs fade
- Bonuses expire
- Nothing is taken away
- Example: Focus buff expires after 48h inactivity; XP, level, items stay
- Psychology: missed opportunity > punishment

**"Welcome back" rewards (VERY effective)**

When users return after absence: bonus XP, special quest, "Glad you're back" message. This reframes absence as normal, not failure.

**Party encouragement, not penalties**

Party members can send encouragement. No group punishment, no "you ruined it for everyone." Social warmth > accountability pressure.

**One sentence summary:** We retain users through gentle rewards and identity-based progress, not punishment, because burnout users disengage when they feel pressure or loss.

### User Stories

Research on user stories for video games, then pick relevant concepts to apply to Degree of Labor. After understanding what user stories are, it's easy to write one on your own — think of scenarios a player/user can do in the app.

**Sample: Stardew Valley User Stories**
- As an unhappy office worker, I want to escape the corporate life of Pelican Town, so that I can find meaning and fulfillment in a simpler, rural setting.
- As a new farmer, I want to restore my grandfather's old farm to its former glory, so that I can honor his legacy and build a thriving life.
- As a new resident of Pelican Town, I want to integrate with the local community, so that I can build relationships, make friends, and potentially start a family.
- As a self-sufficient individual, I want to grow crops, raise animals, and gather resources, so that I can become independent and reduce my reliance on the Joja corporation.

**Degree of Labor User Stories**
- As a user, I want boring real-life tasks to trigger immediate rewards and feedback, so I feel motivated the same way I do when playing video games.
- As a user, I want to see my character level up over time, so my daily effort feels like long-term progress.
- As a user, I want to see my to-do list through quests, so I don't need to organize them.
- Enter the World: Access a landing page that sets the tone and allows me to "Start the Adventure."
- Immerse in the Theme: Move through a pixel-art environment where NPCs (like the Sage or Merchant) offer encouragement, making the act of working feel like a fantasy mission.
- Commit to Quests: Add specific real-world tasks to a "Quest Board" and categorize them by energy level (Low/High) or size (Common/Boss).
- Execute & Reward: Mark tasks as "Slain" to immediately see my XP bar fill and my Gold count increase.
- Analyze Growth: View a detailed character profile that breaks down my "Mastery Distribution," showing me exactly which real-life areas (like Study or Fitness) I am leveling up most effectively.
- Maintain Streaks: Track my "Resilience" and "Streaks" to ensure I don't lose momentum in my real-life habits.

## Verification / Maintenance

DegreeOfLabor is a productivity and motivation-support tool, not a medical or therapeutic intervention. Dopamine and burnout research are used as design inspiration to explain user behavior, not as clinical treatment claims.

- In this project, "dopamine" is used as a simplified model for motivation and reward processing, not as a precise neurological measurement.
- Our primary focus is college and high-school students experiencing burnout-related procrastination. While the system may generalize to other users, our design decisions are evaluated through this group.
- We chose RPG mechanics specifically because they emphasize long-term identity and progression, not short-term points or competition.
- XP is capped per session and designed to reward participation rather than optimization.
